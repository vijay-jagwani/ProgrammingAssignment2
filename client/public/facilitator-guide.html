📦 Supply Chain Game — Facilitator Guide

Everything the person running the game (the “admin”) does, phase by phase, plus a debrief script.

Your role as facilitator

You are not on a team. You set up the game, drive it phase by phase, and — critically — you play the market: you place the customer orders every team must fulfil. Think of yourself as the game master and the customer rolled into one. You can promote co-facilitators to share the load (they also can’t be on a team).

The golden rule: the game only moves when you advance the phase. Give each role a moment to submit, watch for the ✓ marks, then advance. Anyone who doesn’t submit gets a safe default, so a quiet or absent seat never blocks the group.

Before you start (setup)

  • Host a game and choose: number of months (8–10 is a good session), number of SKUs (5 default), customers (how many buyers the market simulates), difficulty, and team budget.
  • Share the 6-character code. Players join, form teams of five, and claim roles.
  • Have co-facilitators join first, then promote them (they must not be on a team). Aim for 2+ teams so the make-vs-buy market between CEOs comes alive.
  • Difficulty: Easy = more capacity than demand — the lesson is that overbuilding bleeds money through age loss, and excess stock must be priced to move. Medium = capacity ≈ demand (95–100%), a near-perfect plan covers everything (recommended). Hard = scarce capacity + volatile demand — no team can make everything, so trading is forced.

What you do each month

Every month runs through eight phases. You drive all of them. Here’s your job at each step:

1
Forecast
Demand Planner acts · you wait & advance
Let each team’s Demand Planner enter a forecast. Watch the ✓ list. When ready (or time’s up), advance.
2
Production
Production Planner acts · you wait & advance
Teams allocate the forecast to lines within capacity. If they try to over-build, the game stops them — that’s the scarcity lesson. Advance when done.
3
Transport
Transport Manager acts · you wait & advance
Teams split each SKU between truckload (arrives now) and interplant (cheaper, arrives next month). Advance when done.
4
Pricing
Customer Ops acts · you wait & advance
Teams set prices above the cost floor. Advancing reveals every team’s prices on the board.
5
Price reveal & trading
CEOs act · you wait & advance
Prices are public. CEOs negotiate make-vs-buy trades with each other. Give them a few minutes — this is the richest discussion of the round. Advance to close the market.
6
Customer orders — this is you
You act
You are the market. The simulation proposes each SKU’s total demand — baseline × number of teams, reacting to prior stockouts and overstock — and you split it across teams. Each team’s price is shown next to its box: reward sharper prices with more volume, or split evenly for identical orders. Month 1: customers want everything on the shelf.
7
Resolution
The engine · automatic on advance
Advancing settles the month: sales, lost sales (stockouts), inventory aging and shelf-life write-offs, holding and transport costs, overdraft interest, and each team’s P&L.
8
Results
Whole team reviews · you facilitate
Teams see forecast vs actual, fill rate, and the P&L breakdown. Pause here and debrief (see below) before starting the next month.

Being the market well

  • Trust the proposal, mostly. The simulated demand already models the bullwhip — stocked-out customers over-order next month, overstocked ones under-order. Confirming it usually gives the best learning.
  • Want drama? Spike or crash one SKU’s order to see who forecasted well and who’s caught with the wrong stock.
  • Let price win volume. You decide how much to buy from each team — shifting volume toward the cheapest shelf is what real customers do, and it makes overstocked teams cut prices to move excess. An even split keeps the classic “fair test” mode.

Debrief script (the learning happens here)

Spend 10–15 minutes per month on Results. Good questions to ask the room:

Forecast vs actual: Who was closest? What did a miss cost you — dead stock, or lost sales?
Bullwhip: Did last month’s stockout make you over-order this month? Watch the swing grow.
Make vs buy: What did you decide to buy instead of make? Was the trade price worth it? Who profited from selling surplus?
Transport: Did anyone get caught with everything on slow interplant and nothing to sell? What’s the right split?
Inventory economics: Who’s bleeding cash on holding, aging, or write-offs? Why?
Teamwork: Five roles, one P&L. Where did the team disagree, and how did the CEO reconcile it?

At the end of the game

  • The final screen ranks teams by cumulative profit, with a cumulative-profit chart and a per-team debrief (stockout losses, biggest forecast miss, trade activity).
  • Close with the big questions: What would you do differently in month 1 knowing what you know now? and Where did cross-functional alignment win or lose the game?
